Tech Billionaires: A New Robber Baron?

Created by Justin Zhai, Ezra Cohen, Mia Cohen, Saurav Patel, Aaron Lewis

In today’s world, growing concern surrounds the widening gap between the ultra-wealthy and the general public.[1] This wage divide is reshaping the fabric of modern American society, deepening economic and social divides.[2] As a group of computer science students, our attention was drawn to what many consider a modern-day Gold Rush and a creator of some of today's most well-known tycoons: the tech industry.

We began by looking at the industry metrics and came discover that that there is a cause for calling the tech industry by that nickname. The data hasn't lied: this industry and the giants within it have grown at a fast pace. Exceeding the growth of non-tech large corporations.

Monthly Visitors at Top 5 Consistently Popular Websites (2010 - 2024)

This stacked area chart compares the monthly active users of sites by popular tech companies (i.e. Google, YouTube, and Facebook) to comparable large internet sites that are not owned by major tech companies (i.e. Wikipedia and Yahoo). The data shows that the monthly visitors of Google, YouTube, and Facebook have grown drastically while the visitors to Yahoo and Wikipedia have declined or largely not kept up with these other sites. This demonstrates a growing dependance on and usage of web applications owned by "big tech" companies, meaning their products become indispensable for daily needs.[7]

When we considered the rising power of the tech industry and public interest in economic inequality, we noticed a recurring theme in conversations about the economy. Issues such as the division of wealth[8], current economic policies (which we do not intend to critique)[9], and concerns over the influence of unelected wealthy individuals in politics[10] have drawn frequent comparisons to a historical period: the Gilded Age.[11] In fact, the current president of the United States has openly stated that, “We were at our richest from 1870 to 1913," directly referencing the period.[12]

When we had these two seemingly unrelated concepts: the almost futuristic wealth from "high tech" and a period over a century ago, we realized that there might be trends or similarites and decided to investigate further. We decided to first consider the period and its context to get a deeper understanding of what we are comparing and contrasting with.
