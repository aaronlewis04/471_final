Part 2: Comparison to Gilded Age

First of all, let's start by discussing what the Gilded Age actually was. Characterized by the period between the post-Civil War era to the early twentieth century off of the heels of the industrial revolution, the name came from author Mark Twain.[13] The reference is to the concept of "gilding," where thin gold leaf is applied to the outer edges of something to make it appear as if it were made of gold. The idea was that the society of the era was gilded: the upper crust led golden lives but underneath it was wood that rotted with nobody knowing.

This lollipop graph shows how the economy was faring in the Gilded Age and compares it to the Contemporary Era. The data was found in a 2007 paper by Hugh Rockoff on the “Great Fortunes Of The Gilded Age”.[14] Due to this the Contemporary Era was 1990-2006 and is not reflective of our current economy. The Gilded Age was a time period of great economic success by every major metric. The cost of living was decreasing, real GDP was increasing, and the stock market was booming. We took inspiration from the lollipop chart in observable plot gallery: Link

But not everything was as great as it seemed. The government's oversight of industry was characterized as "laissez-faire" (to use a French term that means "let them do [what they will]"), meaning that the government was hands-off.[15] Theories of "social Darwinism" pushed an aggresively competetive society, with little aid to those who struggled. It was a perfect foundation for a vast gap in wealth.[16]

These 2 stacked bar charts show the percentage of wealth that the bottom 80%, top 20-5%, top 5-1%, and top 1% had across different years. The data for the Gilded age came from Rockoff's paper[17] and the contemporary data came from the world inequality database.[18] The data for both are very similar, but there are a couple of important differences between them which limit our ability to directly compare them. The first distinction is that the data for the Gilded Age is limited only to Massachusetts, while our contemporary data covers the entire US. The second is that our gilded age data takes place over a period of 90 years in 10 year increments while our contemporary data takes place over a period of 13 years in 1 year increments. The data for the Gilded Age was incredibly limited and this was the best data we could find. In the Gilded Age we can see the portion of the richest grow and the bottom 80% lose more and more of their share of the wealth. In the Contemporary Era we find that all four of the groups stay relatively static in their proportions of the wealth, and while the bottom 80% is doing significantly better than they were in the Gilded Age, the top 1% are as rich now as they were at the height of the Gilded Age. We took inspiration from the histogram chart in observable plot gallery: Link.

When we compared it to today, we were releived to see that modern breakdowns are not nearly as terrible. And an economist we consulted agreed.

"I’d hesitate to attempt to draw parallels between living standards of today and living standards for the average American in the [G]ilded [A]ge," Aidan Owens, UMD Class of 2025, told us. In fact, he said that "Americans are without a doubt living in the most prosperous economy ever." He explained that "The idea goes that, while worker productivity has skyrocketed (which is true), real wages have remained stagnant (also true). The main problem with this analysis is that employees actually make enough to cover the difference, but it’s paid out in non-wage benefits (like way better healthcare and more vacation and sick leave)."[19]

We examined "real wages" (which is wages adjusted for inflation) and CPI (which is, as Owens described, "the average cost of [a] “basket” throughout the years. The basket is just a collection of goods consumers buy" and indicates costs of living)[20] in the recent decades (from the 1970s until this decade) and noticed that it was true: median weekly wages (in "real wages" and therefore actual spending power) has been relatively steady, even rising.

This is a simple progression of the CPI (steadily moving upward with inflation) and the median weekly wages (steady with a slight increase) taken from US Bureau of Labor Statistics.[21]

We concluded that between the economist interview and division of wealth data that looking at lower upper class to lower class would be a bit too complicated, but concluded that many things in this arena are likely better. Instead, this led us to other economic comparisons.

This led our focus to the wealthiest of the wealthy, a comparison that did seem worth pursuing, especially when we have a profile to compare: billionaires.

We have a slew of famous rich tycoons in the Gilded Age, colloquially known as "Robber Barons," who sunk their fingers into politics and are remembered for their unimaginable wealth in comparison to the general public. And in that we found our similarity: tech billionaires.[22]

Part 4: Conclusions

When comparing the contemporary era to the Gilded Age, it's important to recognize that the average American today enjoys a significantly higher quality of life. This improvement isn't solely due to wages, but also to a range of non-wage benefits such as access to healthcare, retirement planning, and a broader array of services. Technological advancements have also played a crucial role, enabling greater focus on family life and contributing to higher levels of education for the average child. However, we can absolutely compare the growth of billionaires’ wealth in the contemporary era compared to the gilded era. Based on our visualizations and data exploration above, we can see that tech billionaires have accumulated an extraordinary amount of wealth over the last decade and a half. But why should we care, and what does that mean for us?

One statement that can be agreed upon by many is that while money might not necessarily buy someone happiness, it can most certainly buy power. In today’s capitalist driven society, having an influential amount of purchasing power can change the lives of many Ameircans indirectly. Wealth allows these tech billionaires to shape public policy, influence elections, and steer the direction of industries through lobbying and campaign contributions. These created policies are not always aligned with the concerns of the average American, rather they are aligned with companies run by the tech billionaires to help both entities make more profit, often with relaxed regulations and tax cuts.

We can see some modern examples of these tech billionaires and their companies influencing aspects of our lives that we interact with on the daily. Below are some interesting sources where you can see these effects taking place:

Jeff Bezos & The Washington Post

Jeff Bezos, the billionaire owner of The Washington Post, has significantly reshaped the newspaper's editorial direction. In early 2025, Bezos oversaw a revamp of the opinion section to emphasize themes aligned with “personal liberties and free markets,” leading to the dismissal of columnists who challenged those perspectives. This shift highlights how immense personal wealth can steer public discourse and frame narratives for millions of readers, reinforcing the idea that money doesn’t just buy influence—it shapes the lens through which society interprets reality.

NPR on Bezos' Washington Post Incident

Elon Musk & Government Policy

Elon Musk’s influence on American policy has grown increasingly visible, particularly in conversations surrounding EV subsidies, infrastructure, and space exploration. Musk's direct communication with the White House and policymakers has helped shape legislation that supports the growth of his companies, like tax credits for electric vehicles and expanded government contracts for SpaceX. While these policies aim to push innovation, they also reflect how the priorities of a single wealthy individual can cascade into nationwide effects—impacting how everyday Americans travel, work, and access technology.

NYT: How Musk Shapes Policy

Microsoft & OpenAI's Governance

Microsoft’s substantial investment in OpenAI has extended beyond funding into strategic control. With a major stake in the company, Microsoft played a key role in influencing board decisions, including the controversial removal and reinstatement of CEO Sam Altman. These moves reflect how corporate power can dictate the direction of cutting-edge technology development. As AI becomes increasingly embedded in everyday tools—from search engines to workplace software—Microsoft’s influence over OpenAI showcases how economic power can shape technological futures for billions, often without broad public accountability.

New Yorker: Microsoft-OpenAI Partnership
